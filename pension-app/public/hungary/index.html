How the Hungary pension system works

Hungary's statutory pension is a proportional defined-benefit PAYG system.

After the abolition of the mandatory funded second pillar in 2010 (assets transferred to the state), the entire mandatory

pension is Pillar 1 PAYG. The accrual rate varies by career length: 33% of average monthly earnings for a 10-year career,

reaching 80% for a 40-year career — giving a high and nearly earnings-independent replacement rate.

The nearly flat replacement rate across earnings multiples (76.9% at 0.5×, 73.4% at both 1.0× and 2.0× AW) reflects

the purely proportional benefit — one of the least redistributive systems in the EU.

Employee contribution: 10% of gross wage; employer: 13%; total: 23%.

Frequently asked questions — Hungary pension

What happened to Hungary's pension funds in 2010?

In 2010 the Orbán government nationalised the mandatory funded second pillar (private pension funds), transferring approximately HUF 3 trillion of accumulated assets to the state. Workers who did not return to the state system lost their private pension rights. Since 2010 all mandatory pension is PAYG Pillar 1 only.

How is the Hungarian pension calculated?

The pension is a percentage of the average monthly earnings over the career (revalued by a wage index). The accrual rate increases with career length: 10 years → 33%, 25 years → 56%, 40 years → 80% of average revalued earnings. Early retirement without penalty requires at least 40 years of service.

Is Hungary's pension inflation-protected?

Hungarian pensions are indexed using the "Swiss indexation" formula: 50% CPI + 50% net wage growth. This partial indexation means that if real wages grow, pensioners receive less than the full real wage growth, gradually eroding the relative value of older pensions.
